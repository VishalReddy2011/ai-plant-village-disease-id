# Disease Overview: Apple Scab
Apple scab is a pervasive fungal disease that significantly affects the plant's foliar canopy and developing reproductive tissues. By inducing severe damage to the leaf epidermis, the disease critically impairs the plant’s photosynthetic capacity. This reduction in the food-making process restricts carbohydrate accumulation, ultimately leading to diminished vegetative growth, poor overall plant health, and unmarketable fruit. In contemporary precision agriculture, the visual diagnostic features of apple scab are frequently analyzed using deep learning models, which demonstrate perfect recall and F1 scores in automated field diagnostics.

## Vulnerable Parts
Leaves and green twigs are consistently the most vulnerable botanical structures exposed to primary infection. During the early spring phenological stages, sepals—the newly emerged leaf buds enclosing the developing floral structures—are typically the very first green parts exposed to overwintering inoculum. The susceptibility of these tissues decreases as they fully expand and mature.

## Symptoms on Leaves
The initial clinical presentation of the infection appears as subtle, water-soaked scratches or light olive-green spots that lack distinct margins. As the pathology advances, these lesions transition from green to dark grey, developing a remarkably soft, velvety appearance on both the adaxial and abaxial leaf surfaces. Over time, these velvety brown to green lesions coalesce, and their margins become sharply distinct against the healthy tissue. The host plant's physiological response to heavy colonization causes the infected leaves to turn highly chlorotic (yellow) and drop prematurely, resulting in catastrophic mid-season defoliation.

## Cause and Spread
The disease cycle is propelled by the continuous production of abundant secondary spores (conidia) within each established leaf injury, driving explosive exponential spread during wet summer months. At the conclusion of the growing season, after leaf fall, the pathogen transitions into a saprophytic phase, continuing to grow and form protective overwintering structures (pseudothecia) within the fallen infected leaves. These decomposing leaves represent the most common and concentrated source of primary reinfection, harboring the fungus safely on the orchard floor until the subsequent spring.

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
The foundational strategy for long-term suppression relies heavily on microclimate modification and inoculum destruction.
* **Pruning:** Trees must be subjected to rigorous structural pruning schedules to establish an open and airy canopy architecture. This facilitates the rapid desiccation of leaves and twigs following rainfall or heavy dew, directly eliminating the prolonged periods of high ambient humidity necessary for spore germination.
* **Orchard Sanitation:** Dropped leaves harboring the overwintering pseudothecia must be meticulously collected and buried or burned.
* **Ploughing:** Mechanical winter ploughing of the orchard floor effectively buries the dropped leaves, accelerating their decomposition.
* **Grazing:** Controlled livestock grazing, particularly allowing sheep to consume the dropped infected foliage, is an effective mechanical intervention to sever the biological disease cycle.

## Management: Chemical
Chemical intervention is a critical component of commercial apple scab suppression. The pathogen exhibits profound evolutionary adaptability; therefore, strict adherence to rotational practices is mandatory to manage resistance.

| Active Ingredient | Application Protocol | Efficacy and Resistance Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Urea (5%)** | Spray 5.4 kg in 50 liters of water during late December. | Applied directly to the orchard floor to accelerate the breakdown of fallen overwintering leaves. |
| **Mancozeb** | Apply 4 g per 1 liter of water. | Broad-spectrum protectant; repeat at strict 7-day intervals during periods of rapid shoot expansion and high precipitation. |
| **Thiophanate-methyl** | Post-blossom spray applications. | High risk of resistance; tolerant strains are widely documented. Must be tank-mixed with alternate FRAC codes. |

> **Safety Warning:** When utilizing any pesticide compound, operators must strictly wear protective clothing and adhere precisely to the instructions detailed on the product label regarding correct dosage, pre-harvest intervals, and safe handling procedures.
