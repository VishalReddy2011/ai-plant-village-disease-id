# Disease Overview: Bacterial Spot
Bacterial spot is a highly destructive bacterial disease of bell pepper that rapidly damages foliage and fruit, often causing severe economic losses under warm and humid conditions. The disease reduces photosynthetic capacity through extensive leaf damage and premature defoliation, exposing developing fruit to direct sunlight and increasing the risk of **sunscald**, which significantly lowers fruit quality and marketability. In modern agricultural diagnostics, Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)-based systems frequently distinguish bacterial spot with high accuracy due to its characteristic lesion appearance.

## Vulnerable Parts
The pathogen primarily infects **young leaves and developing fruit**, entering plant tissues through natural openings such as stomata or through microscopic wounds caused by mechanical injury, rain impact, or abrasion.

## Symptoms on Leaves
Early symptoms appear as tiny, yellow-green, water-soaked lesions, particularly noticeable on the underside of leaves. As the disease progresses, lesions enlarge and darken into brown or black spots with a slightly raised, rough, scabby texture. A bright yellow halo often surrounds infected areas. Severe infections lead to widespread chlorosis, premature leaf drop, and canopy thinning. Fruit symptoms include dark, raised, scabby lesions that reduce appearance, market value, and suitability for fresh-market sale.

## Cause and Spread
The disease is caused by the bacterium *Xanthomonas campestris* pv. *vesicatoria* (along with closely related *Xanthomonas* species in modern classifications). The pathogen is commonly **seed-borne**, allowing spread into new growing regions through contaminated seed or infected transplants. It may also survive in crop debris and soil residues between growing seasons. Warm temperatures, rainfall, overhead irrigation, and splashing water greatly accelerate bacterial dispersal across plant canopies.

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
Preventative sanitation and crop management are essential for reducing disease pressure.

* **Certified Disease-Free Seedlings:** Use only certified, pathogen-free seeds and transplants to prevent introduction into production fields.
* **Crop Rotation:** Rotate away from **Solanaceous crops** (tomato, potato, eggplant, pepper) for multiple growing seasons to reduce residual inoculum.
* **Residue Management:** Immediately destroy or deeply incorporate infected crop debris after harvest to reduce bacterial survival.
* **Irrigation Management:** Avoid overhead irrigation where possible, as water splash significantly enhances bacterial spread.

## Management: Chemical
Chemical suppression is most effective when applied preventatively before severe disease establishment.

| Active Ingredient | Application Protocol | Efficacy and Resistance Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Fixed Copper + Mancozeb** | Preventative sprays every **7–10 days** | Copper provides protective bactericidal activity, while Mancozeb enhances copper effectiveness and persistence. |
| **Oxytetracycline** | High-pressure foliar applications during severe outbreaks | Agricultural antibiotic sometimes used in regions with severe disease pressure or copper resistance; regulatory restrictions may apply. |

Preventative spray programs combined with strict sanitation and irrigation management provide the most effective long-term disease suppression.

> **Safety Warning:** Always follow pesticide label instructions regarding dosage, timing, protective equipment, resistance management, environmental precautions, and pre-harvest intervals to ensure safe and effective disease control.
