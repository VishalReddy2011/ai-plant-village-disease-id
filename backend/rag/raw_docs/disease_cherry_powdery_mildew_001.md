# Disease Overview: Powdery Mildew
Powdery mildew is a widespread fungal disease that significantly impairs vegetative growth, photosynthetic efficiency, and fruit quality in cherry trees. The pathogen primarily infects rapidly growing tissues, causing severe leaf distortion, suppressed shoot elongation, and fruit surface scarring that may reduce commercial value. In modern agricultural diagnostics, deep learning image classification systems frequently identify powdery mildew with exceptionally high accuracy due to its distinctive white fungal growth pattern.

## Vulnerable Parts
The pathogen primarily colonizes young, actively expanding tissues. Emerging leaf buds, tender green shoots, floral sepals, and developing fruit surfaces are highly susceptible to infection. Mature leaves generally exhibit greater physiological resistance and are considerably less vulnerable to primary fungal establishment.

## Symptoms on Leaves
The earliest visible symptoms appear as small, circular patches of white to pale gray powdery fungal growth on the adaxial (upper) surface of leaves. As the infection intensifies, these fungal colonies expand and merge, covering larger portions of the leaf blade with a dense powder-like coating. Young infected leaves often become stunted, brittle, and highly distorted, frequently curling upward or inward along the margins. Severe infections may spread to the abaxial (lower) leaf surface and contribute to reduced canopy development.

## Cause and Spread
The disease is caused by the obligate fungal pathogen *Podosphaera cerasi* (historically referred to as *Podosphaera clandestina* in older literature). The pathogen survives winter through specialized reproductive structures known as **chasmothecia**, which persist within bark crevices and fallen leaf debris. During favorable spring conditions characterized by moderate temperatures and elevated humidity, spores are released to initiate primary infection. Secondary spread occurs rapidly throughout the growing season through continuous production of airborne conidia, enabling repeated cycles of infection.

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
Long-term suppression of powdery mildew relies heavily on canopy management and environmental modification.

* **Pruning:** Winter and summer pruning should be performed to maintain an open canopy structure that promotes sunlight penetration and airflow, reducing humidity favorable for fungal growth.
* **Sanitation:** Fallen leaves and infected plant debris should be removed and destroyed to reduce overwintering inoculum sources.
* **Irrigation Management:** Overhead irrigation should be minimized or avoided, as prolonged humidity and moisture accumulation within the canopy favor fungal proliferation.
* **Canopy Ventilation:** Proper spacing and structural management reduce microclimatic humidity and limit spore establishment.

## Management: Chemical
Preventative fungicide programs should begin shortly after petal fall and continue during periods favorable for disease development. Strict fungicide rotation is strongly recommended due to documented resistance development in several fungicide classes.

> **Safety Warning:** Always follow fungicide label recommendations regarding dosage, spray intervals, protective equipment, environmental safety, and pre-harvest intervals to ensure safe and effective disease management.
