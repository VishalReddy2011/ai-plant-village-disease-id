# Disease Overview: Black Rot
Black rot is one of the most destructive fungal diseases affecting grape production worldwide. Under warm and humid environmental conditions, the disease can spread rapidly and devastate entire grape clusters, leading to severe yield losses and major reductions in fruit quality. By damaging leaves, shoots, and berries, black rot significantly reduces vine productivity and vineyard profitability. In modern agricultural diagnostics, deep learning systems frequently identify grape black rot with high classification accuracy due to its distinctive lesion morphology.

## Vulnerable Parts
The pathogen primarily attacks young, actively growing vine tissues, including newly expanded leaves, tender shoots, tendrils, and floral structures. However, the greatest economic damage occurs in developing grape berries, which remain highly susceptible from bloom until **véraison** (the onset of ripening and color development).

## Symptoms on Leaves and Fruit
Leaf symptoms initially appear as small, circular, light-brown lesions surrounded by dark, sharply defined borders. As lesions mature, tiny black fungal reproductive structures called **pycnidia** develop within the necrotic center, often aligned along leaf veins. On fruit, infection begins as a small pale spot that expands rapidly, eventually consuming the entire berry. Infected grapes become dark, shriveled, and hardened into black, raisin-like **mummies**, which remain attached to the vine or fall to the ground.

## Cause and Spread
The disease is caused by the fungal pathogen *Guignardia bidwellii* (asexual stage: *Phyllosticta ampelicida*). The fungus survives winter primarily within mummified berries remaining on trellises or vineyard floors. During spring rainfall, overwintering structures release spores that infect newly developing vine tissues. Secondary spread occurs rapidly through millions of spores produced inside leaf and fruit lesions, which are dispersed by rain splash and moisture throughout the canopy.

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
Successful long-term suppression of grape black rot depends heavily on sanitation and canopy management.

* **Removal of Mummified Fruit:** All mummified grape clusters should be removed during winter pruning to eliminate overwintering inoculum.
* **Vineyard Floor Management:** Early spring cultivation or shallow tillage helps bury fallen mummies, reducing spore release.
* **Canopy Management:** Shoot thinning, leaf removal around fruit zones, and proper trellis positioning improve airflow and sunlight penetration, reducing humidity favorable for fungal infection.
* **Pruning:** Maintaining an open canopy structure accelerates drying after rainfall and lowers disease pressure.

## Management: Chemical
Preventative fungicide protection is most critical from the **pre-bloom stage through approximately four weeks after bloom**, when developing berries are highly susceptible.

| Active Ingredient | Product Example | Application Protocol | Efficacy and Resistance Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Copper Fungicide** | Various | Preventative sprays before rainfall events | Suitable for organic or low-impact production systems; requires reapplication after heavy rain. |
| **Mancozeb / Captan** | Various | Apply from pre-bloom through early fruit set | Broad-spectrum protectant fungicides that provide strong preventative defense against spore germination. |

Timely preventative fungicide application, combined with rigorous vineyard sanitation, is essential for preventing severe cluster losses during favorable disease conditions.

> **Safety Warning:** Always follow fungicide label recommendations regarding dosage, timing, protective equipment, environmental precautions, and pre-harvest intervals to ensure safe and effective disease management.
