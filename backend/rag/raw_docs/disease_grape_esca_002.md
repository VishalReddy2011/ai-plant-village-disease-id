# Disease Overview: Esca (Black Measles)
Esca, commonly known as **Black Measles**, is a chronic and highly destructive grapevine trunk disease that progressively damages the vine’s internal vascular system. By impairing water and nutrient transport through woody tissues, the disease reduces vine vigor, fruit quality, and long-term productivity. Esca may develop gradually over several growing seasons or, in severe cases, trigger sudden vine collapse (**apoplexy**) during periods of high heat and water demand. In precision agriculture, machine learning systems frequently identify Esca symptoms with high confidence due to its distinctive leaf discoloration patterns.

## Vulnerable Parts
The disease primarily affects the **internal woody structure** of grapevines, including the trunk and cordons, where fungal colonization disrupts vascular function. Although the pathogen remains concentrated in woody tissues, symptoms become highly visible on leaves and fruit clusters due to systemic physiological stress.

## Symptoms on Leaves and Fruit
Esca exhibits highly distinctive foliar symptoms commonly referred to as the **"tiger-stripe" pattern**. Infected leaves develop irregular yellow chlorotic and reddish-brown necrotic streaks between veins, while the major veins remain green. Symptoms often intensify during hot weather or water stress. On fruit, small dark circular spots (**black measles**) develop on berries, which may merge and spread into extensive dark purple or black discoloration. Internally, infected trunks and cordons often display soft white rot surrounded by dark necrotic boundaries when cut open.

## Cause and Spread
Esca is caused by a complex interaction of multiple fungal pathogens rather than a single organism. Primary fungal species commonly involved include *Phaeoacremonium aleophilum* (*Togninia minima*) and *Phaeomoniella chlamydospora*, often followed by secondary wood-decaying fungi. Infection typically occurs through **fresh pruning wounds**, where airborne fungal spores enter exposed vascular tissue. Spores spread through wind, rainfall, and contaminated vineyard debris originating from infected vines.

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
Management of Esca focuses primarily on prevention, wound protection, and removal of severely affected tissues.

* **Pruning Timing:** Delaying pruning until late winter or early spring reduces exposure to peak fungal spore release periods and shortens wound susceptibility.
* **Trunk Renewal:** Infected trunks may sometimes be surgically removed below diseased tissue, allowing retraining of healthy shoots to restore vine structure.
* **Removal of Severely Infected Vines:** Apoplectic or heavily damaged vines should be uprooted and destroyed to reduce vineyard inoculum.
* **Sanitation:** Removing diseased woody debris minimizes sources of fungal spores within the vineyard.

## Management: Chemical
Because Esca pathogens colonize internal wood tissues, curative fungicide treatment is generally ineffective once infection becomes established. Chemical and biological interventions are therefore used primarily as **preventative wound protectants**.

| Active Ingredient / Treatment | Application Protocol | Efficacy and Resistance Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Synthetic pruning wound pastes (e.g., Topsin-M based)** | Apply immediately after dormant pruning | Creates a physical and chemical barrier that protects exposed pruning wounds from fungal invasion. |
| **Trichoderma spp.** | Apply directly to fresh pruning wounds | Biological control agent that colonizes wound surfaces and competitively suppresses pathogen establishment. |

Protective treatments are most effective when applied as soon as possible after pruning, especially within the first **48 hours**, when wound susceptibility is highest.

> **Safety Warning:** When applying pruning wound protectants or agricultural treatments, always follow label instructions regarding dosage, protective equipment, environmental precautions, and safe handling procedures.
