# Disease Overview: Huanglongbing (Citrus Greening)
Huanglongbing (HLB), commonly known as **Citrus Greening**, is one of the most destructive bacterial diseases affecting citrus production worldwide. The disease progressively damages the phloem tissue responsible for transporting nutrients throughout the plant, resulting in severe physiological decline, poor fruit quality, extensive root loss, and eventual tree death. HLB significantly threatens long-term orchard productivity and commercial citrus viability. In precision agriculture, Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)-based diagnostic systems frequently identify characteristic HLB symptoms with exceptionally high classification accuracy. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

## Vulnerable Parts
HLB affects nearly all parts of citrus trees, including leaves, branches, roots, and fruit. The **root system** is particularly vulnerable, often experiencing substantial biomass loss early in infection, which reduces water and nutrient uptake capacity. Young citrus trees are highly susceptible and may decline rapidly if infected soon after planting. Leaves, developing fruit, and structural branches gradually deteriorate due to impaired nutrient transport. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

## Symptoms on Leaves and Fruit
The most recognizable symptom of HLB is **"blotchy mottle"**, an irregular and asymmetrical yellowing pattern that spreads unevenly across leaf veins. Infected trees may also produce bright yellow shoots and display small, upright, stiff leaves resembling nutrient deficiencies such as zinc or iron deficiency. Fruit development becomes severely impaired; infected oranges often remain small, misshapen, asymmetrical, and partially green even at maturity. Fruit quality declines significantly, producing bitter-tasting juice and experiencing high rates of premature fruit drop. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

## Cause and Spread
The disease is caused by the phloem-limited bacterium *Candidatus Liberibacter asiaticus* (CLas). Transmission occurs primarily through feeding by the **Asian citrus psyllid (*Diaphorina citri*)**, an insect vector that injects the bacteria directly into plant tissues during feeding. The psyllid spreads rapidly in warm climates, particularly during periods of new vegetative growth. Human-assisted spread can also occur through grafting infected plant material or movement of contaminated nursery stock. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
Effective management relies on preventing vector transmission and maintaining tree health.

* **Certified Planting Material:** Only disease-free nursery stock and certified budwood should be used for orchard establishment.
* **Vector Exclusion:** Protective screens, exclusion netting, and individual protective covers can reduce psyllid access to young trees.
* **Nutrient Management:** Frequent, low-dose fertilization and fertigation programs support weakened root systems and improve nutrient uptake.
* **pH Management:** Maintaining soil and irrigation water pH between **6.0–6.5** improves nutrient availability.
* **Tree Removal:** Severely infected trees should be removed after insecticide treatment to reduce psyllid dispersal.

## Management: Chemical
Chemical control focuses on suppressing psyllid populations and reducing bacterial pressure within infected trees.

| Active Ingredient / Treatment | Target / Timing | Efficacy Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Imidacloprid (Soil-Applied)** | Target psyllid nymphs during root flush periods | Systemic insecticide providing temporary protection for young citrus trees. |
| **Pyrethrins / Danitol** | Target adult psyllids through foliar applications | Fast-acting insecticides commonly used before removal of infected trees to suppress vector movement. |
| **Oxytetracycline (OTC)** | Direct trunk injection targeting bacterial populations | Emerging treatment used to temporarily reduce bacterial load inside vascular tissues. |

Long-term disease suppression requires integrated management that combines vector control, certified nursery stock, nutritional support, and rapid removal of heavily infected trees. Since no permanent cure currently exists, early detection and prevention remain the most effective management strategies. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

> **Safety Warning:** Always follow pesticide and treatment label instructions regarding dosage, application timing, protective equipment, environmental precautions, and legal usage requirements to ensure safe and effective disease management.
