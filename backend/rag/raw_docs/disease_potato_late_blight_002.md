# Disease Overview: Late Blight
Late blight is one of the most destructive plant diseases in agricultural history and is historically associated with the **Irish Potato Famine**. The disease spreads extremely rapidly under cool, wet environmental conditions and can destroy entire potato fields within days if left unmanaged. By aggressively damaging foliage, stems, and tubers, late blight severely reduces yield, storage quality, and overall crop viability. In precision agriculture systems, deep learning diagnostic models frequently identify late blight symptoms with exceptionally high accuracy due to their distinctive visual characteristics.

## Vulnerable Parts
The pathogen aggressively infects **leaves, stems, petioles, and underground tubers**, making nearly the entire potato plant susceptible. Both above-ground and below-ground tissues can be rapidly colonized during favorable disease conditions.

## Symptoms on Leaves
The earliest symptoms appear as **irregular pale green or water-soaked lesions**, commonly developing near leaf tips or margins where moisture accumulates. Under humid conditions, lesions rapidly enlarge and turn dark brown to black, causing extensive tissue death. A key diagnostic feature that distinguishes late blight from early blight is the appearance of **white, fuzzy fungal-like growth (sporangia)** on the underside of infected leaves, especially at the border between healthy and diseased tissue.

Stem infections often appear as dark brown or black lesions that may girdle stems and collapse foliage. Infected tubers develop **firm, sunken brown lesions** that penetrate deep into internal tissues, frequently leading to secondary bacterial rots accompanied by foul odors during storage.

## Cause and Spread
The disease is caused by the oomycete (**water mold**) *Phytophthora infestans*. Unlike many fungal pathogens, *P. infestans* survives primarily in **living infected tissue**, including contaminated seed potatoes, volunteer plants, and cull piles left after harvest. Disease outbreaks are strongly favored by **cool temperatures, prolonged rainfall, high humidity, and extended leaf wetness**. During favorable conditions, millions of spores are produced and dispersed over long distances by wind and rain, enabling explosive regional epidemics.

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
Effective late blight management requires aggressive sanitation and preventative strategies.

* **Certified Seed Tubers:** Use only certified disease-free seed potatoes to reduce initial inoculum.
* **Cull and Volunteer Management:** Eliminate volunteer potato plants and destroy cull piles before planting to prevent overwintering survival.
* **Canopy Dryness:** Improve field airflow and irrigation timing to reduce prolonged leaf wetness.
* **Vine Desiccation:** Destroy or desiccate potato vines before harvest to minimize spore transfer from foliage to tubers during lifting operations.
* **Field Monitoring:** Monitor regional disease forecasts and scouting reports to identify outbreak risks early.

## Management: Chemical
Chemical management must be **strictly preventative**, as established epidemics become extremely difficult to control.

| Active Ingredient | FRAC Group | Application Protocol and Efficacy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chlorothalonil / Mancozeb** | M5 / M3 | Broad-spectrum protectant fungicides that form the foundation of preventative spray programs; require reapplication after heavy rainfall. |
| **Dimethomorph** | 40 (CAA) | Locally systemic oomycete-specific fungicide effective against *Phytophthora infestans*, often used under elevated disease pressure. |
| **Metalaxyl (Mephenoxam)** | 4 | Historically highly effective systemic treatment, though resistance in some pathogen populations requires cautious use and monitoring. |
| **Copper Formulations** | M1 | Common preventative treatment in organic production systems, though generally less effective than synthetic systemic fungicides during severe outbreaks. |

Successful late blight suppression depends heavily on **early preventative spraying**, regional disease forecasting systems, and immediate response to favorable outbreak conditions.

> **Safety Warning:** Always follow pesticide label instructions regarding dosage, application timing, resistance management, protective equipment, environmental precautions, and pre-harvest intervals to ensure safe and effective disease control.
