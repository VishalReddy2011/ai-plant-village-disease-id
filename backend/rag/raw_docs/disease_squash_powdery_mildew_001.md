# Disease Overview: Powdery Mildew
Powdery mildew is a highly common fungal disease affecting squash under both open-field and greenhouse production systems. The disease reduces plant vigor by coating leaves with dense fungal growth, accelerating premature leaf aging and decreasing photosynthetic efficiency. Severe infections reduce fruit size, total yield, and harvest duration. Premature foliage loss may also expose fruit to direct sunlight, increasing the risk of **sunscald**, reduced rind quality, and poor storage performance, particularly in winter squash varieties.

## Vulnerable Parts
The disease primarily affects **aboveground plant tissues**, including leaves, petioles, and young stems. Crown leaves, shaded lower foliage, and leaf undersides within dense canopies are especially susceptible due to elevated local humidity. Fruit infection is uncommon and generally occurs only under severe disease pressure.

## Symptoms on Leaves
Initial symptoms appear as small, white superficial spots on leaves and stems. These spots expand rapidly into a **grayish-white powdery fungal coating** that develops on both upper and lower leaf surfaces. Yellow chlorotic patches may appear on the upper surface opposite fungal colonies growing underneath. As disease severity increases, infected foliage may become stunted, curled, distorted, yellowed, and eventually dry into brittle brown tissue before dying prematurely.

## Cause and Spread
The disease is primarily caused by the fungal pathogens *Podosphaera xanthii* (formerly *Sphaerotheca fuliginea*) and *Erysiphe cichoracearum*. *Podosphaera xanthii* is generally more aggressive during warm summer conditions, while *Erysiphe cichoracearum* tends to dominate during cooler periods. Large numbers of airborne **conidia (asexual spores)** are produced on infected tissue and dispersed by wind to initiate repeated infection cycles. High humidity promotes spore germination, whereas dry conditions favor spore dispersal and colony expansion. The fungi survive unfavorable periods in crop debris through specialized resting structures called **chasmothecia (cleistothecia)**.

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
Long-term disease suppression depends on canopy management, resistant cultivars, and balanced crop practices.

* **Resistant Varieties:** Select squash cultivars bred with genetic resistance or high tolerance to powdery mildew.
* **Canopy Management:** Maintain proper plant spacing and reduce excessive canopy density to improve airflow and sunlight penetration.
* **Balanced Fertilization:** Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilization, as lush succulent growth is highly susceptible to infection.
* **Overhead Irrigation:** Midmorning overhead irrigation may physically disrupt spores if foliage dries rapidly before evening.
* **Crop Rotation:** Rotate away from cucurbit crops for at least **2 years** to reduce survival of overwintering fungal structures.

## Management: Chemical
Preventative fungicide programs are most effective when initiated **before symptom development**, especially on highly susceptible cultivars. Thorough spray coverage of lower leaf surfaces is critical.

| Active Ingredient | Product Example | Application Protocol | Efficacy and Resistance Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Potassium Bicarbonate** | Carb-O-Nator | Apply **2.5–5 lb/100 gal** every **5–14 days** | Organic-approved contact fungicide with protective and suppressive activity; requires complete coverage. |
| **Tetraconazole** | Mettle 125 ME | Apply **8 fl oz/acre** every **7–10 days** | Systemic DMI fungicide (**FRAC Group 3**); rotate with non-Group 3 fungicides to delay resistance. |
| **Bacillus amyloliquefaciens** | Double Nickel LC | Apply **1–6 quarts/acre** every **3–10 days** | Biological preventative fungicide suitable for organic production systems. |
| **Wettable Sulfur** | Various | Apply labeled preventative rates | Multi-site protectant fungicide; apply before disease establishment and monitor for squash variety sensitivity. |

Preventative treatment, resistant cultivars, and proper canopy airflow together provide the most reliable long-term management of squash powdery mildew.

> **Safety Warning:** Always follow fungicide label instructions regarding dosage, timing, protective equipment, resistance management, environmental precautions, and pre-harvest intervals to ensure safe and effective disease control.
