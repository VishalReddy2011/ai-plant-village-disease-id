# Disease Overview: Leaf Scorch
Leaf scorch is a persistent fungal foliar disease that affects strawberry plants by progressively damaging leaves and reducing canopy health. Severe infections reduce photosynthetic efficiency, limit sugar accumulation, weaken plant growth, and increase vulnerability to environmental stress and secondary pathogens. Although leaf scorch rarely causes complete crop failure, unmanaged infections can significantly reduce plant vigor and fruit productivity over time.

## Vulnerable Parts
The disease primarily targets the **foliar canopy**, but infections may also occur on **petioles, runner stems, stolons, and floral structures** such as pedicels and calyx tissues. Occasionally, green fruit may develop elongated, shallow reddish lesions that generally disappear as berries mature.

## Symptoms on Leaves
Symptoms begin as small, dark purple to reddish-purple spots on the upper surface of leaves. Unlike common strawberry leaf spot, **leaf scorch lesions do not develop pale or white centers**, remaining dark throughout disease progression. As lesions enlarge, tiny black spore-producing structures (**acervuli**) may appear within infected areas, giving a glossy or tar-like appearance. Severe infections cause spots to merge, resulting in widespread reddish-brown discoloration, leaf curling, drying, and a characteristic **scorched appearance**. Flower stalk infections may girdle pedicels, causing flowers and immature fruit to wilt and die.

## Cause and Spread
The disease is caused by the fungal pathogen *Diplocarpon earlianum* (also referred to as *Diplocarpon fragariae*). The fungus survives winter in infected plant debris and dead leaves left within production fields. During spring, spores are produced and spread through **rain splash, overhead irrigation, wind, and physical contact with wet foliage**. Disease severity often increases in older perennial plantings where infected debris accumulates over multiple growing seasons.

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
Long-term suppression of leaf scorch depends on sanitation, canopy management, and preventative growing practices.

* **Certified Planting Material:** Establish fields using disease-free certified transplants or plugs.
* **Resistant Cultivars:** Use resistant strawberry varieties such as **'Hood'** and **'Rainier'** when suitable for local production.
* **Canopy Management:** Proper spacing and runner control improve sunlight penetration and promote rapid leaf drying.
* **Irrigation Management:** Prefer **drip irrigation** where possible. If overhead irrigation is necessary, irrigate during early morning hours and minimize prolonged leaf wetness.
* **Field Sanitation:** Remove infected leaves and plant debris during field renovation. Post-harvest mowing and cleanup help reduce overwintering fungal inoculum.

## Management: Chemical
Preventative fungicide applications should begin during early spring growth and continue through bloom periods, particularly under humid or rainy conditions favorable for disease development.

| Active Ingredient | Product Example | Application Protocol | Efficacy and Resistance Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Copper ammonium complex** | Monterey Liqui-Cop | Apply **1–2 teaspoons per gallon of water** | Contact protectant fungicide that forms a preventative barrier on foliage. |
| **Thiophanate-methyl** | Topsin 4.5 FL | Apply **15–20 fl oz/acre during bloom** | Systemic **FRAC Group 1** fungicide; should be rotated or combined with other fungicides to reduce resistance risk. |
| **Iprodione** | Rovral | Apply according to labeled schedules | Primarily used for gray mold management but also provides secondary suppression of leaf scorch symptoms. |

Combining preventative fungicide programs with proper sanitation and canopy airflow provides the most reliable long-term control of strawberry leaf scorch.

> **Safety Warning:** Always follow pesticide label instructions regarding dosage, timing, protective equipment, resistance management, environmental precautions, and pre-harvest intervals to ensure safe and effective disease control.
