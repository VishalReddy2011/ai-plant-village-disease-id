# Disease Overview: Bacterial Spot
Tomato bacterial spot is a highly destructive bacterial disease capable of causing severe economic losses in tomato production. By triggering rapid foliar damage and premature defoliation, the disease significantly reduces photosynthetic efficiency, weakens plant vigor, and exposes fruit to excessive sunlight, increasing the risk of **sunscald** and market rejection. The disease spreads aggressively under warm, humid conditions and can rapidly affect both greenhouse and field-grown tomatoes.

## Vulnerable Parts
Nearly all **aboveground plant tissues** are susceptible to infection, including leaves, stems, petioles, pedicels, calyx tissues, and developing fruit. Young seedlings are especially vulnerable, particularly in transplant production systems where overhead irrigation facilitates rapid disease spread.

## Symptoms on Leaves
Initial symptoms develop as small, circular, **water-soaked lesions** on lower leaves, often most visible on the undersides of foliage. These lesions darken from pale green or yellow to brown or black, frequently developing a greasy or wet appearance. As disease severity increases, lesions merge into larger necrotic regions accompanied by yellowing tissue. A characteristic **"shot-hole" symptom** may develop when necrotic tissue drops out from the center of older lesions.

Fruit infections appear as small, slightly raised blister-like spots that enlarge into rough, dark, scabby lesions, severely reducing fruit quality and marketability.

## Cause and Spread
The disease is caused by several closely related bacterial species, including *Xanthomonas vesicatoria*, *Xanthomonas euvesicatoria*, *Xanthomonas gardneri*, and *Xanthomonas perforans*. The bacteria survive in contaminated seed, crop debris, volunteer tomato plants, soil, and alternate weed hosts such as nightshade and groundcherry. Disease spreads rapidly through **wind-driven rain, irrigation splash, contaminated equipment, workers handling wet plants, and aerosols**. Warm temperatures (**68°F–86°F**) combined with high humidity strongly favor infection and disease progression.

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
Long-term suppression depends heavily on sanitation, moisture management, and preventing pathogen introduction.

* **Certified Disease-Free Seed:** Use only certified pathogen-free seed and healthy transplants.
* **Seed Treatment:** Treat seeds using **hot water treatment (122°F for 25 minutes)** or approved sodium hypochlorite disinfection protocols.
* **Crop Rotation:** Rotate fields away from **Solanaceous crops** (tomato, pepper, potato, eggplant) for at least **3 years**.
* **Irrigation Management:** Avoid overhead irrigation and use **drip irrigation** to minimize canopy moisture.
* **Field Hygiene:** Avoid pruning, harvesting, or handling plants while foliage is wet.
* **Sanitation:** Destroy infected crop residues after harvest, disinfect tools and seedling trays, and eliminate solanaceous weeds.
* **Mulching:** Apply mulch to reduce soil splash onto lower foliage.

## Management: Chemical
Preventative chemical treatments provide partial suppression, though bacterial resistance—particularly to copper-based compounds—is common.

| Active Ingredient | Product Example | Application Protocol | Efficacy and Resistance Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Copper Hydroxide** | Kocide 3000 | Apply **0.75–1.75 lb/acre** preventatively; repeat every **10–14 days** during favorable conditions | Most effective when tank-mixed with **Mancozeb** to improve performance against copper-resistant bacterial strains. |
| **Acibenzolar-S-methyl** | Actigard 50WG | Apply **0.33–0.75 oz/acre**, ideally within one week after transplanting | Activates plant systemic acquired resistance (**SAR**) and improves disease tolerance. |
| **Bacteriophages** | AgriPhage | Apply foliar sprays **twice weekly**, preferably during evening hours | Biological treatment that directly infects bacterial cells; reduced UV exposure improves effectiveness. |

Integrated disease management combining sanitation, drip irrigation, resistant cultural practices, and preventative sprays provides the most reliable control of tomato bacterial spot.

> **Safety Warning:** Always follow pesticide label instructions regarding dosage, timing, protective equipment, resistance management, environmental precautions, and pre-harvest intervals to ensure safe and effective disease control.
