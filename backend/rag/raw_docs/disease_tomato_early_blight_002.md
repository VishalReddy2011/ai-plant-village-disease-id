# Disease Overview: Early Blight
Tomato early blight is a widespread fungal disease that progressively weakens tomato plants by destroying foliage, stems, and fruit. Although less aggressive than late blight, early blight develops persistently over time, reducing photosynthetic efficiency, weakening plant vigor, and significantly lowering fruit yield and quality. The disease is particularly severe in mature, stressed, or aging plants. In precision agriculture systems, deep learning image classification models frequently identify early blight due to its distinctive concentric lesion patterns.

## Vulnerable Parts
The disease affects **leaves, stems, petioles, and fruit**, typically beginning on older, lower leaves near the soil surface before gradually moving upward through the canopy as plants mature.

## Symptoms on Leaves
Early symptoms appear as small, dark brown to black spots on lower foliage. As lesions enlarge, they develop highly characteristic **concentric rings**, creating a distinct **target-like or bullseye appearance**. Lesions are often surrounded by a bright yellow (**chlorotic**) halo. Severe infections cause lesions to merge, resulting in widespread yellowing, browning, drying, and premature leaf drop.

Stem infections appear as dark, elongated, slightly sunken lesions on stems and branches. Fruit infections commonly develop near the stem attachment point, forming dark, sunken, velvety lesions with concentric rings that reduce fruit quality and marketability.

## Cause and Spread
The disease is caused by the fungal pathogen *Alternaria solani*. The fungus survives between growing seasons in infected crop debris, volunteer tomato plants, contaminated soil, and weed hosts. Spores (**conidia**) spread through **wind, rain splash, overhead irrigation, and soil contact**, particularly during alternating wet and dry conditions. Disease development is strongly favored by **moderate-to-warm temperatures, high humidity, drought stress, and nutrient deficiencies**, especially nitrogen deficiency.

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
Long-term management relies heavily on minimizing plant stress and reducing pathogen exposure.

* **Balanced Nutrition:** Maintain adequate nitrogen and balanced fertilization to delay premature leaf aging and strengthen plant defenses.
* **Staking and Trellising:** Support plants above the soil surface to improve airflow and reduce soil contact.
* **Mulching:** Apply organic mulch (such as straw or grass clippings) to minimize soil splash onto lower foliage.
* **Irrigation Management:** Use **drip irrigation** and water at the base of plants during morning hours to reduce leaf wetness.
* **Sanitation:** Remove and destroy infected plant debris after harvest and disinfect stakes or equipment.
* **Crop Rotation:** Rotate fields away from **Solanaceous crops** (tomato, potato, pepper, eggplant) for **3–4 years**.

## Management: Chemical
Preventative fungicide programs are most effective when initiated before disease establishment, particularly during warm, humid weather favorable for infection.

| Active Ingredient | Product Example | Application Protocol | Efficacy and Resistance Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chlorothalonil** | Bravo Weather Stik | Preventative sprays every **7–14 days** | Multi-site protectant fungicide that provides durable surface protection; most effective before rainfall. |
| **Azoxystrobin + Difenoconazole** | Quadris Top | Foliar application during high-risk weather periods | Systemic fungicide mixture combining **FRAC Groups 11 and 3**, offering preventative and early curative activity. |
| **Bacillus subtilis (QST 713)** | Serenade Garden | Apply according to labeled preventative schedules | Biological fungicide suitable for organic systems; most effective when integrated into a preventative rotation. |

Combining preventative fungicide programs with crop rotation, mulching, staking, and balanced plant nutrition provides the most effective long-term suppression of tomato early blight.

> **Safety Warning:** Always follow pesticide label instructions regarding dosage, timing, resistance management, protective equipment, environmental precautions, and pre-harvest intervals to ensure safe and effective disease control.
