# Disease Overview: Late Blight
Tomato late blight is a highly destructive water-mold disease capable of causing complete crop failure within days under favorable environmental conditions. The disease spreads explosively through tomato fields, rapidly destroying foliage, stems, and fruit. Because of its aggressive nature and rapid reproduction, late blight is considered one of the most economically devastating diseases affecting tomato production worldwide.

## Vulnerable Parts
The pathogen aggressively infects **all aboveground plant tissues** at nearly every developmental stage, including leaves, stems, petioles, branches, and both immature and mature fruit.

## Symptoms on Leaves
Early symptoms begin as **irregular pale green or water-soaked lesions**, often developing near leaf margins or tips where moisture accumulates. Under cool and humid conditions, these lesions rapidly enlarge into dark brown or purplish-black necrotic patches. A key diagnostic feature is the presence of **white, cottony fungal-like growth (sporangia)** on the underside of leaves, especially at the boundary between healthy and diseased tissue during humid weather.

Stem and petiole infections appear as dark, water-soaked lesions that may rapidly girdle tissues, leading to plant collapse. Fruit infections begin as large, firm, gray-green water-soaked spots that later turn into dark brown, leathery lesions. Secondary bacterial infection commonly follows, causing soft rot and foul odors.

## Cause and Spread
The disease is caused by the oomycete (**water mold**) *Phytophthora infestans*. The pathogen survives in living infected tissue, including volunteer tomatoes, infected potato tubers, and discarded crop piles. Spores spread rapidly through **wind, rainfall, humidity, and water splash**, often traveling long distances under favorable weather conditions. Disease outbreaks are strongly associated with **cool temperatures (10°C–28°C), prolonged leaf wetness, fog, rainfall, and relative humidity exceeding 90%**.

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
Long-term suppression relies heavily on sanitation, moisture management, and rapid elimination of infection sources.

* **Certified Planting Material:** Use only certified disease-free seeds and transplants.
* **Volunteer Plant Removal:** Destroy volunteer tomatoes, potatoes, and cull piles before planting.
* **Canopy Aeration:** Prune lower leaves and stake or trellis plants to improve airflow and speed foliage drying.
* **Irrigation Management:** Avoid prolonged moisture and overhead irrigation; use **drip irrigation** where possible.
* **Sanitation:** Immediately remove and destroy infected plants and nearby contaminated debris.
* **Crop Rotation:** Rotate away from **Solanaceous crops** (tomato, potato, pepper, eggplant) for **3–4 years** in high-risk areas.
* **Soil Treatment:** Consider soil disinfection or solarization in fields with a history of severe outbreaks.

## Management: Chemical
Because late blight develops extremely rapidly, fungicide applications must be **strictly preventative** and initiated before widespread symptom development.

| Active Ingredient | Product Example | Application Protocol | Efficacy and Resistance Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chlorothalonil / Mancozeb** | Various | Apply preventative label rates and reapply after heavy rainfall | Broad-spectrum multi-site protectants that form the foundation of late blight prevention programs. |
| **Mandipropamid** | Revus | Apply foliar sprays every **7–10 days** during favorable conditions | Specialized **FRAC Group 40 (CAA)** oomycete fungicide with strong protective and antisporulant activity. |
| **Copper Hydroxide** | Champ WG | Apply according to labeled preventative schedules | Copper-based protectant commonly used in organic systems; generally less effective during severe epidemics. |

Rapid preventative intervention guided by weather forecasts and local disease monitoring networks is essential for successful suppression of tomato late blight.

> **Safety Warning:** Always follow pesticide label instructions regarding dosage, timing, resistance management, protective equipment, environmental precautions, and pre-harvest intervals to ensure safe and effective disease control.
