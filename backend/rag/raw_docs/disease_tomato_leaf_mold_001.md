# Disease Overview: Leaf Mold
Tomato leaf mold is a highly infectious fungal disease that primarily affects tomato foliage, especially in **greenhouse and high-tunnel production systems** where humidity remains elevated. The disease reduces photosynthetic efficiency by damaging leaf tissues and interfering with gas exchange, ultimately weakening plant vigor, reducing fruit quality, and lowering yield. Severe infections can result in substantial defoliation and production losses.

## Vulnerable Parts
The disease primarily affects the **leaf canopy**, usually beginning on older, lower leaves and progressively moving upward through the plant. Under severe disease pressure, **blossoms, stems, and fruit** may also become infected.

## Symptoms on Leaves
Initial symptoms appear as small, pale green or yellowish spots on the **upper leaf surface**, often lacking sharply defined margins. As infection progresses, these spots enlarge and become bright yellow. On the **underside of leaves**, directly beneath affected areas, a highly characteristic **velvety olive-green to brown fungal growth** develops.

As lesions expand and merge, leaves become necrotic, curl, wilt, and eventually die, although infected leaves often remain attached to the plant. Blossom infections may cause flowers to blacken and drop prematurely. Fruit infections, though less common, appear as **dark, leathery, sunken lesions near the stem end**.

## Cause and Spread
The disease is caused by the fungal pathogen *Passalora fulva* (formerly *Fulvia fulva* or *Cladosporium fulvum*). The fungus survives between growing seasons in infected crop debris, soil, and contaminated greenhouse materials as mycelium, spores (**conidia**), or hardened survival structures (**sclerotia**). Spores spread through **wind, water splash, contaminated tools, workers' clothing, insects, and greenhouse airflow**.

Disease development is strongly favored when **relative humidity exceeds 85%**, especially when leaf surfaces remain moist. The pathogen enters leaves directly through **stomata**, where fungal growth interferes with normal gas exchange.

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
Effective management focuses heavily on reducing humidity and improving canopy airflow.

* **Resistant Cultivars:** Plant resistant tomato varieties such as **'Red Mountain', 'Primo Red', 'Mountain Spring', 'Geronimo', 'Trust', and 'Boa'** where appropriate.
* **Humidity Management:** Maintain greenhouse relative humidity below **85%** using fans, vents, and proper ventilation.
* **Condensation Prevention:** Maintain nighttime temperatures to reduce dew and moisture formation on foliage.
* **Irrigation Management:** Use **drip irrigation** and avoid wetting foliage whenever possible.
* **Canopy Ventilation:** Space, stake, trellis, and prune plants to improve airflow and reduce humidity pockets.
* **Sanitation:** Sterilize tools, trellises, and supports using **10% bleach solution**, and remove infected debris after harvest.
* **Seed Management:** Use certified seed or apply **hot water seed treatment (122°F for 25 minutes)** when appropriate.

## Management: Chemical
Preventative fungicide programs are highly effective, especially in enclosed environments where humidity favors disease development.

| Active Ingredient | Product Example | Application Protocol | Efficacy and Resistance Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Tetraconazole + Azoxystrobin** | Affiance | Apply **9–12 fl oz/acre** every **7–14 days** | Dual-action systemic fungicide highly effective against leaf mold in greenhouse and tunnel production systems. |
| **Copper Hydroxide** | Champ WG | Apply labeled greenhouse preventative rates | Organic-approved protectant fungicide requiring thorough coverage, especially on leaf undersides. |

Maintaining low humidity and initiating preventative fungicide applications before symptom establishment provide the most reliable long-term suppression of tomato leaf mold.

> **Safety Warning:** Always follow pesticide label instructions regarding dosage, timing, resistance management, protective equipment, environmental precautions, and pre-harvest intervals to ensure safe and effective disease control.
