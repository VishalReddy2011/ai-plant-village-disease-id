# Disease Overview: Target Spot
Tomato target spot is a fungal disease that has become increasingly problematic in warm, humid tomato-growing regions. The disease damages leaves, stems, and fruit, significantly reducing photosynthetic capacity and lowering crop yield. Severe fruit infections result in blemishes and cracking that greatly reduce marketability, especially for fresh-market tomatoes.

## Vulnerable Parts
The pathogen can infect nearly all **aboveground plant structures**, including **leaves, stems, petioles, peduncles, pedicels, sepals, and fruit**. Both vegetative and reproductive tissues are susceptible under favorable environmental conditions.

## Symptoms on Leaves
Leaf symptoms initially appear as **small, round dark brown to black lesions**, often surrounded by a distinct **yellow chlorotic halo**. As lesions enlarge, they develop highly characteristic **concentric rings**, creating a **target-like appearance** that may resemble early blight symptoms.

Stem, petiole, and pedicel infections appear as dark, elongated lesions. As disease severity increases, infected leaves become yellow, necrotic, and eventually drop, leading to severe canopy blighting and defoliation.

Fruit symptoms begin as **small raised blister-like lesions** that enlarge, darken, and become **slightly sunken, rough, and cracked**, often displaying concentric ring patterns that reduce fruit quality and marketability.

## Cause and Spread
The disease is caused by the fungal pathogen *Corynespora cassiicola*. The fungus survives between growing seasons in infected crop debris, volunteer plants, and alternative hosts due to its broad host range. Disease development is strongly favored by **warm temperatures, prolonged humidity, and abundant rainfall**. Spores spread rapidly through **wind, rain splash, irrigation water, and mechanical contact during field operations**.

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
Successful long-term management depends heavily on sanitation and moisture reduction.

* **Certified Planting Material:** Use healthy, disease-free seeds and transplants.
* **Crop Rotation:** Rotate fields with **non-solanaceous crops** to reduce pathogen survival.
* **Sanitation:** Remove volunteer tomato plants, weeds, and infected crop residues after harvest.
* **Irrigation Management:** Avoid overhead irrigation and prefer **drip irrigation** to minimize leaf wetness.
* **Canopy Ventilation:** Proper spacing, staking, and trellising improve airflow and speed canopy drying.
* **Avoid Wet Handling:** Avoid pruning, harvesting, or tying plants while foliage remains wet to reduce pathogen spread.

## Management: Chemical
Preventative fungicide programs are most effective when initiated before favorable infection periods, especially after humid or rainy weather.

| Active Ingredient | Product Example | Application Protocol | Efficacy and Resistance Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chlorothalonil** | Bravo Weather Stik | Preventative sprays every **7–14 days** | Multi-site protectant fungicide that forms a durable protective barrier before rainfall or high humidity periods. |
| **Boscalid + Pyraclostrobin** | Pristine | Apply **10.5–14.5 oz/acre** according to label schedules | Dual-action systemic fungicide (**FRAC Groups 7 + 11**) providing strong preventative and early curative suppression of target spot and related foliar diseases. |

Combining preventative fungicide use with strong sanitation, drip irrigation, and canopy airflow management provides the most effective long-term suppression of tomato target spot.

> **Safety Warning:** Always follow pesticide label instructions regarding dosage, timing, resistance management, protective equipment, environmental precautions, and pre-harvest intervals to ensure safe and effective disease control.
