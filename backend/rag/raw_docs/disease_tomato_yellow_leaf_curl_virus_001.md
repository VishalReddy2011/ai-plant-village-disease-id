# Disease Overview: Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus
Tomato yellow leaf curl virus (TYLCV) is one of the most economically devastating viral diseases affecting tomato production worldwide. The virus disrupts photosynthesis, nutrient transport, and normal plant growth, causing severe stunting, flower abortion, poor fruit development, and potentially **complete crop failure**, particularly when infection occurs during early growth stages. Because infected plants cannot be cured, prevention and vector management are critical.

## Vulnerable Parts
The virus affects nearly all **aboveground plant tissues**, including **young leaves, stems, flowers, and developing fruit**. Early vegetative growth stages are particularly vulnerable, as infection during seedling establishment or early canopy formation causes the greatest yield losses. Root growth and overall plant vigor may also become severely impaired.

## Symptoms on Leaves
The most recognizable symptom is **severe upward curling and cupping of leaves**, especially in young foliage. Infected leaves often become **yellow between veins (interveinal chlorosis)**, thickened, wrinkled, and leathery in texture. Plants typically exhibit **severe stunting**, shortened internodes, excessive branching, and a compact, bushy appearance.

Reproductive symptoms are severe and include **flower abortion, delayed flowering, poor fruit set, and development of small, misshapen, or unmarketable fruit**. Plants infected early may produce little to no harvestable yield.

## Cause and Spread
The disease is caused by **Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus (TYLCV)**, a single-stranded DNA virus belonging to the *Begomovirus* genus within the *Geminiviridae* family. The virus is transmitted primarily by the **silverleaf whitefly (*Bemisia tabaci*)**, which spreads TYLCV while feeding on plant sap.

Additional transmission pathways include **infected transplants, grafting material, and contaminated seedlings**. The virus survives between seasons in alternate host plants such as peppers, potatoes, eggplants, and numerous weeds. Disease spread is strongly favored by **hot, dry climates**, which accelerate whitefly population growth and viral transmission.

## Management: Cultural and Mechanical
Because infected plants cannot be cured, prevention and vector exclusion are essential.

* **Resistant Cultivars:** Grow tomato varieties carrying **Ty resistance genes (Ty-1 to Ty-6)** whenever available.
* **Certified Planting Material:** Use only virus-free, whitefly-free seed and transplants.
* **Physical Exclusion:** Install **insect-proof netting, row covers, or greenhouse screening** to prevent whitefly entry.
* **Reflective Mulches:** Use reflective plastic or aluminum-coated mulch to repel flying whiteflies.
* **Vector Monitoring:** Deploy **yellow sticky traps** to monitor and suppress whitefly populations.
* **Field Sanitation:** Immediately remove and destroy symptomatic plants and maintain strict weed control to eliminate alternate virus reservoirs.
* **Host Separation:** Avoid planting tomatoes near susceptible alternate hosts such as peppers or eggplants.

## Management: Chemical
There is **no chemical cure or viricide** capable of eliminating TYLCV from infected plants. Chemical management focuses entirely on **controlling whitefly populations** to reduce virus transmission.

| Active Ingredient | Product Example | Application Protocol | Efficacy and Resistance Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Imidacloprid (Systemic)** | Admire Pro | Soil or drip application during early growth stages | Systemic insecticide providing preventative suppression of whitefly populations on young plants. |
| **Spiromesifen** | Oberon 2SC | Apply **7.0–8.5 fl oz/acre** as foliar spray | **Group 23** insecticide targeting whitefly eggs and immature stages. |
| **Horticultural Oils** | Various | Apply foliar sprays every **7–10 days** | Soft-contact option that interferes with whitefly feeding and reduces vector activity. |

Integrated management combining **resistant cultivars, whitefly exclusion, early vector control, sanitation, and clean planting material** provides the most effective long-term defense against TYLCV.

> **Safety Warning:** Always follow pesticide label instructions regarding dosage, timing, pollinator protection, resistance management, protective equipment, environmental precautions, and pre-harvest intervals to ensure safe and effective pest control.
